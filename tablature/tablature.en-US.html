Accordion Diatonic Tablature

Diatonic accordion tablature represents a valuable and accessible tool for musical initiation, especially for those without traditional training in sheet music reading. Before addressing the specifics and benefits of tablature for this peculiar instrument, it's important to understand what it means and how it differs from conventional music notation.

What is Tablature?

Tablature is an alternative form of music notation intended to simplify the learning of an instrument. Unlike traditional sheet music, which requires knowledge of music notation to interpret notes and rhythms, tablature provides direct indications on how to play the instrument. In the context of the diatonic accordion, this involves indicating which button to press and, in some cases, whether the bellows should be open or closed.

Specificity of the Diatonic Accordion

The diatonic accordion differs from other instruments, such as the piano, due to its unique organization of notes. While the piano presents a linear sequence of notes, from low to high, the diatonic accordion organizes the notes in a less intuitive way for beginners. This characteristic makes tablature particularly useful for this instrument, as it can guide the learner on which button to press without the need to understand the complex arrangement of notes on the instrument.

Limitations of Tablature

Despite its advantages in facilitating initial learning, tablature for the diatonic accordion has limitations. One of the main ones is the absence of indication about the duration of the notes. While traditional sheet music provides a complete understanding of the musical piece, including rhythm and dynamics, tablature mainly focuses on the location of the notes. This means that musical interpretation and expressiveness can be compromised if the musician relies exclusively on tablature.

Another aspect is that tablature is generated, particularly, for each instrument. For example, suppose there is a "G" note in a given score. The tablature will indicate exactly which button should be pressed on this instrument, let's say, the fifth button of the inner row. If we change instruments, it may be that the same "G" note is present in a completely different position or it may not even exist on this new instrument.

Diversity and Challenges of Tablature Models

Tablature stands out for its simplified language, a less formal alternative to the conventional notation found in sheet music. This simplicity allows tablature models to take on varied forms, reflecting the flexibility and adaptability of this notation system. Although this variety brings the advantage of decentralizing the representative process, making tablatures potentially more accessible to different learning styles, it can also introduce significant challenges. The diversity of formats, while reflecting the customization of learning, can complicate the exchange of information between musicians. Each variation of tablature, being unique to the instrument or to the individual preference of the musician, may not be immediately understandable to others, requiring a period of adaptation or even the search for a common denominator in terms of graphical representation and symbols used.

Importance of Standardization

Standardizing basic elements in tablatures can be an effective solution to facilitate communication and exchange of musical pieces between musicians from different schools. The creation of a set of guidelines or universal symbols that allow clear representation of specific actions, such as the direction of the bellows in the diatonic accordion, button and row numbering, as well as the way to represent bass buttons, among others, is proposed. This approach would maintain the accessible and uncomplicated nature of tablatures, while providing a common basis to significantly improve the exchange of musical information. It is important to note that this standardization would not eliminate customization and flexibility, characteristics that make tablatures especially attractive to musicians.

Tablature Formats Used

Note about the bass:

In our project, for any tablature format, the bass will always be represented by the "Chord Notation System". We understand that when dealing with diatonic accordions, we are talking about a very limited number of buttons in the bass section (usually 8 or 12), making them easy to memorize. Some tablature systems number both the melody lines (right hand) and the bass lines (left hand).

Another peculiarity of the bass buttons is that they can represent individual notes or chords (which can be major or minor). For these representations, we have adopted the following patterns:

  • Bass note: Uppercase letter - e.g., Note “A”, letter “A”

  • Major chord: Lowercase letter - e.g., Major chord “A”, letter “a”

  • Minor chord: Lowercase letter and hyphen - e.g., Minor chord "A minor”, letter “a-”

In our "Map for Diatonic Accordions", we have chosen 3 different formats to offer some different options to those learning the instrument and, at the same time, not create too many differentiations. These are:

  • German Format

  • Cyclical Numeric Format

  • Continuous Numeric Format

Note: For each of them, there may be variations, as we will explain below.

German Format

This format is called "German Format" because it has been derived from the teachings of Yann Dour, in his book "Schule für Diatonisches Akkordion – Band 1". Essentially, the buttons in each row are numbered, from top to bottom, that is, from the chin towards the knees, from 1 to "n", where "n" represents the last button of the row. Apostrophes "'" are used to differentiate the rows, where the first row (the outermost one) does not have any apostrophe; the second row has one character; the third row has two characters "''" and so on, adding an extra apostrophe to each new row.

In the tablature, we will use numbers (with or without apostrophes) to represent the melody notes and letters from the "Chord Notation System" to represent the bass.

Bissonority

One fundamental characteristic of the diatonic accordion still remains to be mapped. Bissonority, meaning the functionality that each button has to emit two different sounds: opening the bellows plays one note or chord and, closing the bellows, another different one.

In our Map, we use split circles to represent the accordion buttons. Always, the upper half of the button represents the note (or chord) when the bellows are closed and the lower half, the note (or chord) that sounds when opening the bellows.

Finally, The Tablature: Bringing it all together

Now that we have learned how to number the melody buttons and how to represent the bass, let's see how we will combine all this in the tablature.

Accordion tablature can be well represented by a table with 3 rows:

  • The top row represents the bass notes (left hand).

  • The second and third rows display the buttons played with the right hand, where the second row shows which buttons should be pressed with the bellows closing and the third row shows the buttons pressed with the bellows opening.

  • The vertical lines represent the bar lines.

Despite the simple design, many pieces of information are represented simultaneously. Note that, in the example above, we have the representation of two measures.

In the first one, G basses are played (G and g), respectively, the note G and the G Major chord, while button number 6 of the first row (outer) is pressed, with the bellows closing.

The second measure indicates the execution of D basses (D and d), respectively the note D and the D Major chord, also pressing, simultaneously, button number 6 of the first row (outer), with the bellows opening.

Cyclic Numeric Format

This new format, which we call Cyclic Numeric, eliminates the need to number the rows because they are numbered starting with a different decade each. Of course, the rows can have more than 10 buttons, which would cause the repetition of button numbers. This, in fact, happens, but can be overcome with some trickery, such as adding an additional character to differentiate the buttons with repeated numbers.

Continuous Numeric Format

The third (and last) format that we present was inspired by the numbering that some manufacturers use to differentiate the buttons.

Starting from the outermost button, at the top, count from 1 to "n", with "n" being the last button of the innermost row. Thus, the need to use extra characters to differentiate buttons is eliminated, but, like the cyclic numeric format, in this format some problems can also appear depending on the keyboard layouts. Other Elements of the Tablature

As seen in the tablature excerpt above, some extra elements can appear.

In the first measure, for example, there are no bass sounds; only melody notes sound, represented by 2 touches on button 16. Note that, in the first third of the measure, there is no sound (it is a pause). Thus, to represent the pause, a bar, similar to a dash "–", was used.

Another very common element is the sound continuity symbol. For example, in the second measure, the A minor chord "a-" starts with the note from button 16 and should last during the execution of this note and the next (button 15). An arrow "→" is used to represent this continuity. That is, the A minor chord is played along with note 16 and this will be maintained until the time of note 15 ends.

Chord Notation System

The "Chord Notation System" is a globally recognized convention for the notation of musical chords, used to simplify the reading and sharing of musical compositions among musicians from different languages and cultures. This system uses letters from the Latin alphabet to represent the chords, accompanied by numbers and symbols that indicate modifications, such as minor, augmented, diminished chords, and other variations.

Here, in the Map, we use this convention to represent not only chords but also musical notes. As we know, the monosyllabic indicators of sound pitch were introduced by Guido d'Arezzo, are predominantly used in Latin-speaking countries and correspond to the seven letters (alphabetic system introduced by Pope Gregory I in the 6th century) used in English, German, Greek, etc. They are:

Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Si
English C D E F G A B
German C D E F G A H

Note: In English, the letter "B" represents the note "B"; in German, the letter "B" represents the note "B flat," and "B" is represented by "H." In this project, we use the English nomenclature.

Numbers and symbols are used to indicate specific alterations in basic chords, such as 7 (seventh), m (minor), or # (sharp), among others. However, diatonic accordions with 8 or 12 bass buttons have only major and minor chords. As mentioned earlier, here we use uppercase letters to represent notes and lowercase letters for chords. When it comes to minor chords, we opt for the hyphen "-" instead of the letter "m".
